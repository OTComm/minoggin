Summary

Demonstrative adjectives directly modify nouns to show the distance relationship between the speaker and the noun being modified or to clearly indicate the topic of discussion.

Explanation

Demonstrative adjectives directly modify nouns to show the distance relationship between the speaker and the noun being modified or to clearly indicate the topic of discussion. Demonstrative adjectives include "this," "that," "these," and "those."

Examples

  1. {This} pizza doesn't look quite as good as {that} pizza, but if {these} seats were expensive, imagine how much {those} seats cost.
  2. {This} sauce is amazing.
  3. I enjoyed {that} movie.
  4. If {these} keys are mine, then whose are those? (Notice that "those" is not followed by a noun that it modifies. Here, "those" is functioning as a demonstrative pronoun, standing in place of "keys" to avoid having to repeat the word.)
  5. You could't pay me to take {those} tickets.

Grammar Tips

Adjectives modify and pronouns replace. To tell the difference between a demonstrative adjective and a demonstrative pronoun, simply check if the noun it corresponds to immediately follows it. If the corresponding noun immediately follows, then it is modifying the noun and is a demonstrative adjective. If the noun does not follow it, then it is standing in place of it and is a demonstrative pronoun.
