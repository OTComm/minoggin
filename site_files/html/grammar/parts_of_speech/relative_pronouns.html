Summary

Relative pronouns connect extra information that is related to a noun or pronoun to the rest of the sentence.

Explanation

Relative pronouns are used to link a dependent clause to a main clause, providing more information about a noun or pronoun mentioned in the sentence. Words like “who,” “whom,” “whose,” “which,” and “that” are all relative pronouns. Each serves a specific function based on the type of noun it relates to:

  • “Who” refers to people and acts as a subject.
  • “Whom” also refers to people but serves as an object.
  • “Whose” indicates possession.
  • “Which” refers to animals or things, often introducing non-restrictive clauses that are not essential to the sentence's meaning.
  • “That” can refer to people, animals, or things and introduces restrictive clauses essential to the sentence’s meaning.

Examples

  1. She knows {who} the book belongs to.
  2. She knows to {whom} the book belongs.
  3. She knows {whose} book it is.
  4. The book, {which} I had been meaning to read for some time, is missing.
  5. The book {that} I left on the table is missing.
