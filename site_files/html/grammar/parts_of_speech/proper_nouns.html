Summary

Proper nouns name unique entities, including individuals, formal titles, specific organizations, or geographical locations, and are always capitalized.

Explanation

Proper nouns name unique entities, including individuals, formal titles, specific organizations, or geographical locations, and are always capitalized. Unlike common nouns, which refer to a class or category of things, proper nouns specify unique, singular entities that are one of a kind within the context they are used.

Examples

  1. Listen, {Jeff}, if I were the {Mayor of Saint Paul} I would let this slide, but I need those reports done before the mayor shows up.
  2. Listen, {Jeff}, if I were the mayor I would let this slide, but I need those reports done before {Mayor Olson} shows up.
  3. I used to be a {Windows} guy, but {Apple} has far superior user interface design.

Grammar Tips

Proper nouns are always capitalized. However, the capitalization of titles like “doctor” or "president" depends on word order and context. In the sentence, “The doctor who will see you today is Henry Wilson,” “doctor” is a common noun and not capitalized, despite being preceded by the definite article. This contrasts with “The Doctor of Advanced Medicine at the University of Minnesota will see you today,” where "doctor" forms part of a specific, formal title and is thus capitalized. Similarly, “I am pleased to inform you that Doctor Henry Wilson will see you today,” shows “doctor” used as part of a formal title, and therefore, it is capitalized.
